Alexander Fleming

Discoverer of Penicillin

Alexander Fleming was a Scottish bacteriologist who made one of the most significant discoveries in the history of medicine. In 1928, he discovered penicillin, the world's first antibiotic, which has saved millions of lives since its development. His work led to the development of antibiotics that revolutionized the treatment of bacterial infections.

Key Achievements

  • 1881
    Began education at Louden Moor School, Scotland.
  • 1895
    Attended Regent Street Polytechnic in London for further education.
  • 1906
    Graduated from St. Mary's Hospital Medical School, London, with a degree in medicine.
  • 1921
    Discovered lysozyme, an enzyme with mild antibacterial properties, while researching bodily secretions, which set the foundation for his later work on penicillin.
  • 1928
    Discovered the antibiotic properties of Penicillium notatum.
  • 1930s
    Published various research papers on bacteriology and immunology, emphasizing the use of antiseptics.
  • 1940s
    Revolutionized the treatment of bacterial infections, saving countless lives during World War II and beyond.
  • 1942
    Penicillin was used for the first time to treat bacterial infections in a patient.
  • 1944
    Knighted by King George VI for his contributions to medicine.
  • 1945
    Awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine, shared with Howard Florey and Ernst Boris Chain.
  • 1955
    Passed away at his home in London at the age of 73.
  • 1955
    Buried at St. Paul's Cathedral in London, a rare honor for a scientist.
  • 1999
    Named in Time magazine's list of the 100 Most Important People of the 20th century.
